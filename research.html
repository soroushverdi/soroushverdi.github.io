Overview of research

My research program is organized around the idea that simple models, when carefully constructed, can provide deep insights into complex systems. I work at the interface of theory, methodology, and application, with projects ranging from conceptual modeling to empirical validation.

A central theme is the development of interpretable frameworks that remain faithful to real-world data while preserving mathematical and conceptual simplicity. This includes work on:

  • Designing minimal models that exhibit realistic behaviors.
  • Developing explanatory tools for communicating model assumptions.
  • Studying robustness and sensitivity of simplified systems.

Another focus area concerns the pedagogical value of simple models. I investigate how stripped-down examples can be used to teach complex concepts in undergraduate and graduate curricula, and how they can support students in building intuition before encountering more advanced techniques.

I also collaborate with researchers in neighboring fields, applying these ideas to domains such as social systems, networked infrastructures, and computational humanities. In these collaborations, my work often centers on aligning modeling assumptions with substantive domain knowledge.

Future projects plan to extend these foundations to:

  • Cross-disciplinary modeling toolkits for teaching and research.
  • Open, reproducible repositories of simple benchmark models.
  • Methodological guidelines for balancing simplicity and realism.
